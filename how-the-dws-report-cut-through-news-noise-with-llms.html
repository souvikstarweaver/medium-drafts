How the DWS Report Cut Through News Noise with LLMs

A case study in using large language models to score, deduplicate, and decay content for better news curation

Most news aggregators drown their readers. They pull everything, rank nothing, and call the resulting firehose "comprehensive."

The DWS Report took the opposite approach. Instead of aggregating more, the team built a system that aggregates less, on purpose, using large language models to decide what stays, what gets removed, and what fades over time. The result is a news feed that treats reader attention as a finite resource and curates content to match.

Three mechanisms make the system work: LLM-based relevance scoring, embedding-driven deduplication, and time-based decay. Each one solves a distinct failure mode in traditional content curation.

How LLM Scoring Replaces Keyword Matching

Traditional news aggregation relies on keyword frequency and source authority to rank articles. An article about "AI regulation" rises to the top because it contains the right words from a recognizable publisher. The problem: keyword matching cannot distinguish between a meaningful policy analysis and a rewritten press release. Both contain the same terms. Both rank the same.

The DWS Report replaced keyword matching with LLM-based relevance scoring DWS REPORT, 2026. Each incoming article gets evaluated by a language model that reads the full text, not a bag of extracted keywords, and assigns a relevance score based on topical depth, novelty, and alignment with the publication's editorial focus.

Think of it like the difference between a metal detector and a gemologist. The metal detector finds everything that pings. The gemologist tells you which stones are worth keeping.

The LLM scores articles on a continuous scale rather than a binary pass/fail. Articles that score below a threshold never reach the reader. Articles that score high get priority placement. The scoring model learns from the editorial patterns of the publication itself, so its definition of "relevant" stays calibrated to what the DWS Report's audience cares about.

This is the first cost reduction. Every article that gets filtered out before publication is an article that no human editor had to review, tag, or reject.

Deduplication Through Embeddings, Not Headlines

Scoring determines what is relevant. Deduplication determines what is redundant.

News breaks once. Coverage follows in waves. A single event can generate dozens of articles across publishers within hours, each one restating the same facts with minor variation. Traditional aggregators catch exact duplicates by matching headlines or URLs. They miss the near-duplicates, the articles that say the same thing in different words.

The DWS Report stores each published article as an embedding, a numerical fingerprint that captures the article's meaning rather than its surface text DWS REPORT, 2026. When a new article arrives, its embedding gets compared against every article in the database. If the similarity score crosses a threshold, the new article gets flagged as a near-duplicate and suppressed.

This is the equivalent of comparing two photographs by what they show, not by their file names. Two articles with different headlines, different bylines, and different opening paragraphs can still be recognized as covering the same story.

The user experience impact is direct. Readers see one version of a story instead of five. The feed stays dense with distinct information instead of padded with restatements. For a reader spending fifteen minutes on a news roundup, every redundant article is a slot stolen from something new.

From an operations standpoint, deduplication compounds the cost savings from scoring. Fewer articles pass the relevance filter. Fewer of those survivors make it past the deduplication check. The volume of content that requires any human attention shrinks at each stage.

Time-Based Decay Keeps the Feed Current

Relevance is not permanent. An article that scored high on Monday morning can become stale by Wednesday. Policy announcements get superseded. Earnings reports lose urgency after the market reacts. The news cycle moves, and a static score does not move with it.

The DWS Report introduced a decay mechanism that reduces an article's effective score over time DWS REPORT, 2026. Fresh content enters at full strength. As hours and days pass, the score diminishes according to a decay curve calibrated to the topic's typical shelf life. Breaking news decays fast. Analysis and long-form reporting decay slow.

This is how a grocery store rotates stock. New inventory goes to the front. Older inventory moves to the back. Items past their useful date get pulled from the shelf entirely. No one has to walk the aisles and check expiration dates by hand. The system handles rotation automatically.

The decay mechanism also solves a subtle curation problem: topic fatigue. Without decay, a high-scoring article about a trending topic can dominate the feed for days, crowding out new stories on different subjects. Decay ensures that even strong articles yield their position to fresher material over time.

For teams considering this approach, the decay curve is the design decision that matters most. Too aggressive, and quality analysis disappears before readers find it. Too slow, and the feed starts to feel stale. The DWS Report calibrates decay rates by content category, which means the system treats a feature investigation differently from a product launch announcement DWS REPORT, 2026.

What This Means for Teams Building Content Products

The DWS Report is a small operation. That is part of the point. The combination of LLM scoring, embedding deduplication, and time-based decay allowed a lean team to run a news aggregation product that would have required a full editorial staff under traditional methods.

The broader pattern here is worth naming. Content curation used to be a labor problem. You needed humans to read, judge, filter, and sequence articles. Each of those tasks now has an LLM-native equivalent that runs at a fraction of the cost per unit. LLMs are already reshaping how readers discover and consume content across industries Arc Intermedia, 2026.

The operational savings are real, but the bigger win is editorial consistency. Human editors have good days and bad days. They have blind spots and favorites. An LLM scoring model, once calibrated, applies the same judgment to article number one and article number ten thousand.

None of this removes the need for editorial oversight. The LLM scores. A human still sets the scoring criteria, audits edge cases, and adjusts the decay curves when the model drifts. The role of the editor changes from "read everything and pick the best" to "define what best means and verify the system is applying it."

For any PM or founder building a content product, the DWS Report's architecture is a template worth studying. Not because the specific tools are novel, but because the sequencing is right: score first, deduplicate second, decay third. Each stage reduces the volume that the next stage has to process. That compounding reduction is where the cost savings live.
