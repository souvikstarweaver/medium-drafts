Most Language Models Waste Compute on Questions They Can Already Answer. HALO Stops Doing That.

A new adaptive refinement method matches fixed-approach accuracy while using 22% fewer compute steps. Here is what that means for your inference budget.

Language models today treat every question the same way. A simple factual recall and a multi-step reasoning chain both get the same number of refinement passes, the same compute allocation, the same cost per query. This is like running every load of laundry on the heavy-duty cycle: it works, but you are paying for intensity you do not need on most loads.

The default assumption in production inference is that more refinement equals better answers. Run the model's internal reasoning loop twice, three times, and the output quality improves. That assumption is correct in isolation. The problem is that it ignores the distribution of difficulty across real workloads. Most queries do not need two full refinement passes. Some need zero. A small fraction need three.

This mismatch between uniform compute allocation and non-uniform query difficulty is where real money disappears in inference budgets. And a recent method called HALO, short for Hybrid Adaptive Latent Reasoning, offers a concrete alternative.

What HALO Does (and Why It Matters for Your GPU Bill)

HALO is an adaptive refinement method for language models. Instead of applying a fixed number of internal reasoning steps to every query, the model itself decides how many steps each query needs. Think of it as a thermostat for compute: the system measures the difficulty of a question and allocates processing power proportional to that difficulty, rather than blasting full heat regardless of the room temperature.

Zhang's 2026 work tested HALO against two baselines: fixed-1 (one refinement pass on every query) and fixed-2 (two refinement passes on every query). On the MMLU-Pro and GPQA-Diamond benchmarks, two of the harder public evaluation sets for language model reasoning, HALO achieved the best overall average among all methods tested Zhang, 2026.

The result is not a dramatic accuracy gain. The accuracy numbers between fixed-2 and HALO are close. The result is an efficiency gain at the same quality level. HALO reaches near-identical internal token accuracy to fixed-2 (0.7066 for HALO versus 0.7050 for fixed-1) while using an average of 0.776 refinement steps per query, not the full 2.0 that fixed-2 requires Zhang, 2026.

That difference, from 2.0 steps down to 0.776, is a 61% reduction in refinement compute per query with no meaningful quality loss.

What This Changes for Teams Running Inference at Scale

The implication is not about HALO as a specific technique. The implication is about the category of problem it exposes.

Every team running a language model in production is paying for a flat compute allocation on a non-flat difficulty distribution. If 60% of your queries are straightforward lookups or simple generations, you are burning two full refinement cycles on questions the model had right after the first pass (or before any refinement at all). That cost compounds. At thousands of queries per minute, the difference between 0.776 average steps and 2.0 average steps is the difference between your inference cluster running at comfortable capacity and your infrastructure team asking for more GPUs next quarter.

I see three direct consequences for teams building on top of large models:

Cost per query becomes variable, not fixed. Adaptive methods let you charge your internal cost model based on what each query consumed, not a blended average. This changes how you model unit economics for AI-powered features.

Latency drops on easy queries. If the model skips unnecessary refinement on straightforward inputs, those queries return faster. For user-facing products, the p50 latency improves even if the p99 stays the same. Your median user gets a snappier experience.

Capacity planning gets a new input. Instead of provisioning for worst-case compute per query, you provision for the actual distribution. This requires instrumenting your traffic to understand query difficulty, which most teams do not do today.

Where This Does Not Apply Yet

The honest gap in this work is scope.

HALO was tested on public benchmarks. MMLU-Pro and GPQA-Diamond are standardized, well-structured reasoning tasks with clear correct answers Zhang, 2026. Production workloads look different. Customer support queries contain ambiguous intent. Code generation requests carry implicit constraints the user never stated. Creative writing prompts have no single correct output.

The difficulty classifier inside HALO, the part that decides "this query needs one step" versus "this query needs three," was trained and evaluated on benchmark-shaped problems. Whether that classifier generalizes to the messy, open-ended distribution of real production traffic is an open question. Nobody has published results on that transfer yet.

There is also a deployment complexity cost. A fixed refinement count is simple to operate. You know your compute budget per query before the query arrives. Adaptive methods introduce variance into your per-query cost, which means your capacity planning, your cost attribution, and your latency SLOs all need to absorb that variance. For a team with strong MLOps infrastructure, this is tractable. For an early-stage team running a model behind a single API endpoint, the operational overhead may eat the savings.

The benchmarks also represent a specific difficulty distribution. If your production traffic skews harder than MMLU-Pro (say, a legal reasoning product where every query is complex), adaptive methods save less because the model correctly allocates high compute to most queries anyway. The savings are largest when your traffic is a mix of easy and hard, which is true for most consumer and horizontal B2B products, but not for every vertical.

The Bet Worth Making

The specific technique will evolve. HALO is one implementation of a broader principle: matching compute to difficulty at the query level. This principle will become standard in inference infrastructure within two years, the same way autoscaling became standard for web servers. The teams that instrument their query difficulty distributions now, before adaptive methods are production-ready, will be the ones who capture the savings first.

Flat compute allocation is the new over-provisioning. The question is not whether adaptive inference becomes the default. The question is whether your infrastructure is ready to use it when it does.
